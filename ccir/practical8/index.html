Statistical inference

See Example 7.1 of Boyd & Vandenberghe "Convex Optimization" book.

Suppose we observe yi=ai⊤x+viy_i = a_i^\top x + v_iyi​=ai⊤​x+vi​ where viv_ivi​ has a normal distribution N(μi,σi2)\mathcal{N}(\mu_i, \sigma_i^2)N(μi​,σi2​).

We want to recover xxx from the observation yiy_iyi​ such that the viv_ivi​ are likely to occur. That is, we want to maximize f(vi)f(v_i)f(vi​). This is multi-objective. Instead, we can maximize the likelihood that they all occur. Since they are independent, it's the product.

∏i=1nf(vi)=∏i=1n1σi2πexp⁡(−(vi−μi)22σi2)\prod_{i=1}^n f(v_i) = \prod_{i=1}^n \frac{1}{\sigma_i \sqrt{2\pi}}\exp(-\frac{(v_i - \mu_i)^2}{2\sigma_i^2})i=1∏n​f(vi​)=i=1∏n​σi​2π​1​exp(−2σi2​(vi​−μi​)2​)

Since log⁡\loglog is an increasing function, that is equivalent to maximizing the logarithm:

∑i=1nlog⁡(1σi2πexp⁡(−(vi−μi)22σi2))\sum_{i=1}^n \log(\frac{1}{\sigma_i \sqrt{2\pi}}\exp(-\frac{(v_i - \mu_i)^2}{2\sigma_i^2}))i=1∑n​log(σi​2π​1​exp(−2σi2​(vi​−μi​)2​))

which is equal to

−∑i=1nlog⁡(σi)−nlog⁡(2π)2−12σi2∑i=1n(vi−μi)2-\sum_{i=1}^n \log(\sigma_i) - \frac{n\log(2\pi)}{2} -\frac{1}{2\sigma_i^2} \sum_{i=1}^n (v_i - \mu_i)^2−i=1∑n​log(σi​)−2nlog(2π)​−2σi2​1​i=1∑n​(vi​−μi​)2

The first two terms do not depend on viv_ivi​ so we can drop them.

−12∑i=1n(vi−μi)2σi2-\frac{1}{2} \sum_{i=1}^n \frac{(v_i - \mu_i)^2}{\sigma_i^2}−21​i=1∑n​σi2​(vi​−μi​)2​

−12-\frac{1}{2}−21​ is a negative constant so maximizing this expression is equivalent to minimizing

∑i=1n(vi−μi)2σi2\sum_{i=1}^n \frac{(v_i - \mu_i)^2}{\sigma_i^2}i=1∑n​σi2​(vi​−μi​)2​

In terms of xxx, this is

min⁡x∑i=1n(yi−ai⊤x−μi)2σi2\min_x \sum_{i=1}^n \frac{(y_i - a_i^\top x - \mu_i)^2}{\sigma_i^2}xmin​i=1∑n​σi2​(yi​−ai⊤​x−μi​)2​

If μi=0\mu_i = 0μi​=0 and σi\sigma_iσi​ does not depend on iii (same for all samples, we say they are independent and identially distributed (i.i.d)), this gives

min⁡x∥y−Ax∥2\min_x \lVert y - Ax \rVert_2xmin​∥y−Ax∥2​

where the iiith row of AAA is aia_iai​. So the classical linear regression we saw during the first week assumes i.i.d. normal noise of zero mean.

How to interpret the scaling σi2\sigma_i^2σi2​ in terms of influence on xxx for very noisy samples ?

n = 101
σ = [1000; ones(n - 1)]
μ = 100rand(n)
v = randn(n) .* σ .+ μ

m = 10
x = rand(m)
A = rand(n, m)
y = A * x + v

Without taking the noise into account, we get large errors:

x - A \ y

Taking μ\muμ into account can be done as follows:

x - A \ (y - μ)

How do we take σ\sigmaσ into account ?

We know that \ solves the least square

min⁡x∥y−Ax∥2=∑i=1n(yi−ai⊤x)2\min_x \lVert y - Ax \rVert_2 = \sum_{i = 1}^n (y_i - a_i^\top x)^2xmin​∥y−Ax∥2​=i=1∑n​(yi​−ai⊤​x)2

but we want it to solve

∑i=1n(yi−ai⊤x−μi)2/σi2\sum_{i = 1}^n (y_i - a_i^\top x - \mu_i)^2 / \sigma_i^2i=1∑n​(yi​−ai⊤​x−μi​)2/σi2​

instead. How do we do this ? The expression is equal to

∑i=1n(yi−μiσi−ai⊤σix)2\sum_{i = 1}^n (\frac{y_i - \mu_i}{\sigma_i} - \frac{a_i^\top}{\sigma_i} x)^2i=1∑n​(σi​yi​−μi​​−σi​ai⊤​​x)2

so we can just scale yyy and AAA:

x - (A ./ σ) \ ((y - μ) ./ σ)

Now that's much better.
